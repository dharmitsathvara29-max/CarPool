=====================================================
PART 1 — WHAT WE'RE BUILDING
=====================================================

a terminal-based INTERCITY CARPOOLING app, in C++, for the DSA mini-project.

the idea: 5 cities, 5 drivers (one parked in each city). a customer says
"i'm in city 2, take me to city 5". they get matched to a driver, share the
ride with up to 2 other people going roughly the same way, the app picks the
best route, splits the cost fairly, and shows the whole thing animating in
the terminal.

it is NOT a ride-hailing app (no door-to-door pickup). it's CARPOOLING:
everyone meets at a city's pickup point, the car fills up, then it goes.
this is the choice that keeps the routing simple and the project finishable.

the whole thing runs live in the terminal — cars actually move on screen,
seats actually fill, time actually passes. that's what makes the demo good.

every data-structure / algorithm the project requires has a real job here.
nothing is bolted on just to tick a box:
  - QUEUE   -> hands out seats fairly (first come first served)
  - STACK   -> per-person payments, and undoing them if one fails
  - GRAPH   -> the city map
  - DIJKSTRA-> shortest path between cities
  - BFS/DFS -> BFS checks a city is reachable before Dijkstra runs; DFS lists
               all paths. (on the tree: inorder = DFS, level-order = BFS too)
  - GREEDY  -> chaining shortest paths to cover all 3 destinations
  - BST     -> driver leaderboard (who served the most riders) + O(log n) search
  - SORTING -> rank drivers/rides (and we compare 3 algorithms for the report)
  - SEARCH  -> linear + binary, to find drivers
  - ARRAYS / LINKED LIST / FILE I/O -> store drivers, history, save & load

PART 2 — THE RULES OF THE WORLD (memorize these)
=====================================================

these are fixed numbers everyone shares. they live in config.h. nobody
hardcodes them anywhere else — you include config.h and use the name.

  SPEED        = 2 km per sim-minute       (every car, fixed)
  TIME_SCALE   = 2.5 sim-minutes per 1 real second
  RATE         = 21 rupees per km          (full taxi rate)
  SEATS        = 3 per driver
  DRIVERS      = 5
  WAIT_TIMEOUT = 10 sim-minutes a half-empty driver waits before leaving

things that fall out of those numbers (handy for sanity checks):
  - a car moves 2 x 2.5 = 5 km on screen per real second
  - the 10-minute wait = 10 / 2.5 = 4 real seconds
  - a 30 km trip takes 15 sim-min = 6 real seconds

IMPORTANT: the rate is 21, NOT 4. earlier drafts said 4 — that's dead.
21/km is the number. it only exists in config.h.


=====================================================
PART 3 — THE FULL JOURNEY, STEP BY STEP
=====================================================

this is what happens from the moment the app opens. screens the user SEES
are marked [SCREEN]. steps that happen invisibly behind a screen are marked
[BEHIND]. (the flowchart picture shows this same thing — purple = screen,
teal = behind, amber = the decision.)

STEP 0  [SCREEN] HOME
   the map is drawn in ascii. cars are already moving around on their own —
   filling up, driving, dropping off, coming back. this is the "ambient
   simulation" (see PART 4.5). it's alive before the user does anything.
   user presses a key to start booking.

STEP 1  [SCREEN] MAP SELECT
   the user navigates the cities with ARROW KEYS (not by typing a number).
   a highlight box moves between cities; ENTER confirms. this uses raw
   terminal mode (termios) + ANSI escape codes for the live cursor feel.

STEP 2  [BEHIND] ASSIGN SEAT
   the instant they confirm a city, the app pops a seat off the QUEUE and
   assigns it to them. their seat is now reserved. (the queue is how we stay
   fair — whoever asked first gets the next free seat.)

STEP 3  [SCREEN] SEAT SCREEN
   shows: which driver you got, which seat is yours, and the other 2 seats
   filling up live (from other real users, or from random ambient riders).
   you watch the car fill.

STEP 4  [BEHIND, every tick] THE DECISION — does the car leave?
   checked constantly while the car waits:
     - all 3 seats full           -> LEAVE NOW
     - 10 min passed AND >=1 rider -> LEAVE with whoever's aboard
                                      (empty seats go back to the queue)
     - 10 min passed AND 0 riders  -> CANCEL, car stays, no route computed
   (in the ambient home sim this is what makes cars leave half-full sometimes
   — that's intentional, it looks real.)

STEP 5  [BEHIND] ROUTE COMPUTED
   now we know the destinations (1 to 3 cities). we run DIJKSTRA + GREEDY
   to find the shortest route that covers all of them, starting from where
   the car is. (see PART 4.3.) this gives us the path and the total km.

STEP 6  [BEHIND] FARES SPLIT
   using the route, we calculate each rider's fair share with the
   SEGMENT formula (see PART 4.4 — this is the clever bit). each person's
   fare gets pushed onto the payment STACK.

STEP 7  [SCREEN] RIDE SIM
   the car animates along its route, the chosen path drawn in a different
   colour. live clock. when the distance travelled reaches the total km,
   the ride is done.

STEP 8  [SCREEN] PAYMENT
   shows the user their fare and a [Pay] button. clicking it pops their
   payment off the stack and marks it paid. (if a multi-person payment ever
   fails partway, the stack unwinds in reverse — that's WHY it's a stack.)

STEP 9  back to HOME. the driver returns to its home city, its 3 seats go
   back into the queue, its "rides served today" goes up by one (that feeds
   the leaderboard / dashboard). the loop continues.


=====================================================
PART 4 — THE FIVE IDEAS YOU MUST UNDERSTAND
=====================================================

if you get these five, you understand the whole app. everything in the
spec is just these ideas turned into functions.

--- 4.1  THE SEAT QUEUE (fairness) ---
all the seats from all drivers go into one queue at startup:
   [D1-S1, D1-S2, D1-S3, D2-S1, D2-S2, D2-S3, ... ]  (5 x 3 = 15 slots)
a booking POPS the front slot. when a trip finishes, those 3 seats get
PUSHED back to the rear. when a half-empty car leaves, its unused seats
get pushed back too. first-come-first-served, automatically. that's the
entire point of using a queue here.

--- 4.2  THE DEPARTURE DECISION (the timer) ---
the moment a car's FIRST seat fills, a timer starts (we stamp the sim-time).
every tick we check: full? or has 10 min passed? this is the amber diamond
in the flowchart. the key wiring detail: the function that fills a seat
(P2) WRITES the start-time; the simulation (the team lead's tick) READS it.
two people's code touching the same field — get the field name right.

--- 4.3  THE ROUTE (dijkstra + greedy) ---
dijkstra finds the shortest path between TWO cities. but a full car has up
to 3 destinations. so we chain it greedily:
   start at the car's city
   repeatedly: of the destinations not yet visited, go to the NEAREST one
               (dijkstra to it), then continue from there
   until all destinations are covered
that produces one full path covering everyone, and the total km.

--- 4.4  THE FAIR FARE (the segment formula) — the heart of the project ---
naive idea: charge everyone the same per-km. UNFAIR — someone going a short
hop pays the same rate as someone going the whole way, and if one person
rides a lonely stretch alone the driver loses money.

the fix: charge RATE per km on each ROAD SEGMENT, then SPLIT that segment's
cost among ONLY the people actually in the car on that segment.

   fare(person) = SUM over every segment they ride of:
                      RATE x segment_km / (number of people aboard that segment)

worked example — route A->B->C->D, 10 km each, RATE 21:
   riders:  C1: A->D   C2: A->C   C3: A->B

   segment A-B (10km): C1,C2,C3 aboard (3) -> 21x10/3 = 70 each
   segment B-C (10km): C1,C2 aboard (2)    -> 21x10/2 = 105 each
   segment C-D (10km): C1 alone (1)        -> 21x10/1 = 210

   C3 pays 70      (short hop, shared the whole time -> cheap)
   C2 pays 175     (70 + 105)
   C1 pays 385     (70 + 105 + 210; rode the lonely tail alone -> full rate there)

   driver earns 70x3 + 105x2 + 210 = 630 = 21 x 30km  (ALWAYS full fare)

why it's fair: you get the discount only WHILE you're actually sharing.
ride alone -> pay full. if all 3 ride the whole way, every segment has 3
people, so everyone pays 21/3 = 7 per km — the simple split, as a special
case. the driver always collects exactly RATE x total_km. guaranteed.

--- 4.5  THE AMBIENT SIM (random life on the home screen) ---
on the home screen nobody's really booking, so we GENERATE fake riders so
the cars move on their own. the world is "full ambient": cars fill, depart,
drive the real dijkstra route, complete, return home, repeat — all using the
exact same tick() and assignSeat() as the real flow. we just feed it random
input instead of keyboard input.

calm pacing: every ~45 sim-minutes (~18 real sec) we roll a dice, and ~40%
of the time a random rider appears (random source city, random different
destination). riders trickle in; cars often leave half-full on the timeout;
different cars are in different states at any moment. that's what sells it.

two gotchas:
  - call srand(time(0)) ONCE at the top of main(). never inside a loop, or
    "random" returns the same value forever.
  - when a REAL user is booking, their chosen seat is reserved for them; the
    random riders just fill whatever's left. same queue, same function.

PART 5 — HOW THE CODE IS SPLIT
=====================================================

file tree (each .cpp has a matching .h with just the declarations — that's
what others #include to call your functions without seeing your code):

  carpool/
  ├── main.cpp              LEAD: screen state machine + glue
  ├── config.h             shared constants (everyone reads, nobody fights)
  ├── types.h              shared structs (THE single source of truth)
  │
  ├── datastore.{h,cpp}    P1: driver array, linked-list history, file save/load
  ├── seatqueue.{h,cpp}    P2: seat allocation queue (FIFO)
  ├── paymentstack.{h,cpp} P2: per-person payment stack (pay + rollback)
  ├── sorting.{h,cpp}      P3: merge / quick / insertion + timing comparison
  ├── search.{h,cpp}       P3: linear + binary search
  ├── leaderboard.{h,cpp}  P3: BST of drivers by rides served
  ├── graph.{h,cpp}        LEAD: city graph + dijkstra
  ├── routing.{h,cpp}      LEAD: multi-dest greedy route + segment fares
  ├── simulation.{h,cpp}   LEAD: time tick + car movement + ambient spawn
  └── ui.{h,cpp}           LEAD: termios raw mode + ANSI rendering
  data/  drivers.txt, history.txt

how the pieces depend on each other (top = independent, bottom = where it
all comes together):

       config.h + types.h          <- everyone reads, nobody edits alone
              |
   +----------+--------------------+-------------+
 datastore  sorting/search/board   graph          (these know nothing
 (P1)       (P3)                    (LEAD)          about each other)
                                      |
                                   routing (LEAD)  <- needs graph
                                      |
        +-----------+-----------------+
   seatqueue/paystack ---- simulation
     (P2)                    (LEAD)
                               |
                              main (LEAD)   <- the ONLY place everything meets

the important takeaway: most modules are ISLANDS. sorting doesn't know the
map exists. dijkstra doesn't know about seats. that's deliberate — it means
you can build and test your part ALONE, with fake data, without waiting on
anyone. all the messy "connecting" lives in simulation.cpp and main.cpp,
which the lead owns.


=====================================================
PART 6 — HOW WE ACTUALLY WORK TOGETHER
=====================================================

THE ONE RULE: match the INPUT and OUTPUT in the spec. inside your function,
do whatever you want — your own variable names, your own logic, your own
helper functions. nobody reviews your internals. they only plug into your
input/output.

WHY THIS WORKS:
  - everyone codes at the same time, in their own file. no blocking.
  - integration is just calling functions. if it breaks, we know whose.
  - you can prove your part works without anyone else's code (see below).

TEST WITH FAKE DATA — this is how you know you're done before integration:
make up an input by hand, run your function, check the output matches what
the spec's test case says. example for sorting:

  Driver fake[5] = { {id:1,rides:7}, {id:2,rides:2}, {id:3,rides:9}, ... };
  mergeSortDrivers(fake, "rides");
  // expect rides in order: 2, 7, 9, ...

if your fake test passes, your function works in the real program. you never
needed to see anyone else's file.

THE ONE THING WE MUST AGREE ON FIRST: types.h and config.h.
every "fake data" test assumes everyone agrees what a Driver looks like. the
MOMENT someone quietly adds a field, the fake data drifts and integration day
turns into a nightmare. so: we freeze types.h and config.h in the meeting,
and after that nobody edits them alone — any change is announced to everyone.

SHARING CODE: use github (one repo, a branch each, or just folders per person
to start). don't email .cpp files around.

PART 7 — WHAT TO PREPARE, BY ROLE
=====================================================

read your row, then go read your functions in carpool_code_spec.txt.

P1 — DATASTORE (arrays, linked list, files)
  your job: define how a Driver/Booking is stored, build the 5 drivers,
  keep ride history in a linked list, save everything to a text file and
  load it back identical.
  prepare: how you'll lay out a driver in a text line, and how you'll parse
  it back. practice file read/write. your stuff is an island — test it with
  fake drivers, save, load, check they're the same.
  functions: initDrivers, addToHistory, saveDrivers, loadDrivers,
             saveHistory, loadHistory.

P2 — SEATQUEUE + PAYMENTSTACK (queue + stack)
  your job: the seat queue (pop on booking, push back on trip-end/timeout)
  and the payment stack (push each fare, pop to pay, roll back if one fails).
  prepare: get comfortable with queue and stack operations. understand the
  ONE wiring detail — assignSeat must set the driver's wait-start-time and
  status when the first seat fills, because the lead's tick() reads those.
  read PART 4.1 and 4.2 above twice.
  functions: initQueue, assignSeat, returnSeats, countFilled,
             pushPayments, processNext, rollbackAll.

P3 — SORTING + SEARCH + LEADERBOARD (sorts, search, the BST)
  your job: implement merge sort, quick sort, insertion sort (all sorting
  drivers by a chosen field), a timing comparison that prints which is
  fastest with Big-O, linear and binary search, AND the BST leaderboard
  (insert drivers keyed on rides-served, in-order traversal gives them back
  ranked). the BST is what satisfies the project's "tree" requirement.
  prepare: know all three sorts cold (you'll explain them in the viva).
  for binary search remember the precondition: the array must be sorted by
  the key first. for the BST, practice insert + three walks: inorder (ranking,
  also counts as DFS), level-order (row-by-row, counts as BFS, uses a queue),
  and searchDriver (find a driver in O(log n)). pure island — test everything
  with a fake driver array, no other files needed.
  functions: mergeSortDrivers, quickSortDrivers, insertionSortDrivers,
             compareTimings, linearSearchByCity, binarySearchById,
             insertDriver, inorder, levelOrder, searchDriver.

LEAD (you) — SIMULATION + UI + MAIN + GRAPH + ROUTING
  your job: the whole connected core plus the routing math. the city graph,
  dijkstra between two cities, the greedy multi-destination route, and the
  SEGMENT FARE formula (PART 4.4 — the centerpiece, be ready to explain it).
  then the tick that advances time and moves every car, the departDriver
  burst (collect destinations -> route -> fares -> push to P2's stack),
  completeTrip, the ambient random spawn, all the termios/ANSI rendering,
  and the main state-machine loop.
  this is every connection point, the routing algorithms, and the screen
  drawing — the heaviest plate, but it's the part where everything meets, so
  it has to be the one person who holds the whole picture.
  prepare: dijkstra with a priority queue; BFS (reachability) and DFS
  (all-paths) on the graph; greedy nearest-neighbour chaining;
  reproduce the 385/175/70 fare example by hand; termios raw mode + ANSI.
  functions: buildGraph, dijkstra, bfsReachable, dfsAllPaths, multiRoute,
             segmentFares, isOnSegment, tick, departDriver, completeTrip,
